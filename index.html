How I'm preparing for the work ahead

I've been thinking carefully about the day-to-day of an examiner, the meetings, the reports, the conversations with institution staff, and where I can sharpen my skills before stepping into the role. Three areas have my focus:

Writing with clarity

Findings only matter if they read cleanly. I'm working on writing precise, well-organized examination notes and reports, the kind that communicate a concern plainly and stand up to review.

Speaking with people

Examinations run on conversations with management and staff. I'm practicing how to ask clear questions, listen carefully, and discuss sensitive findings with professionalism and tact.

Gathering the right information

Strong meetings start with preparation. I'm building the habit of identifying what to request, what to review, and what to verify before an important discussion, so I walk in ready.

These are the skills I see at the core of effective examination work, and I'm committed to strengthening them from day one.

Long-term goals & certification path

I see this position as the start of a credentialed examiner career, not a stop along the way. With the Division's support for training and certification, here's the trajectory I'd commit to:

Security+

CompTIA Security+

The industry-standard baseline credential. It validates the core security fundamentals every examiner should command and is a natural first step on the path.

CISA

Certified Information Systems Auditor

The cornerstone credential for IT examination and audit. My top priority, it maps directly to the verify-and-assess work at the heart of this role.

CISSP

Certified Information Systems Security Professional

To deepen the security-domain expertise behind every control I'd evaluate, broadening the technical lens I bring to examinations over time.

CRISC

Certified in Risk & Information Systems Control

A risk-focused credential that complements the examiner's mandate, sharpening how I assess and communicate IT and operational risk across institutions.

From foundation to specialization, each of these is an investment I'd want to repay through sharper, more credible examination work for the Division.

Aligned with how the Division works

Independence

Objectivity over advocacy

An examiner's value is impartial judgment. I'm drawn to the discipline of assessing institutions against the standard, not steering them, but holding the line on safety and soundness.

Public trust

Protecting depositors

Behind every IT control is someone's money and data. The mission of safeguarding the institutions the public relies on is exactly the kind of work I want to do.

Rigor

Evidence, not assumption

Good examination is documented and defensible. I'd rather verify a control with evidence than take a process on faith, the habit your team described resonated with me.

Compliance

Grounded in the GLBA

The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act sets the standard for how institutions protect customer information. I'm prepared to assess compliance with its Safeguards Rule, Financial Privacy Rule, and Pretexting Provisions as a core part of every examination.

How I'd approach the first year

First 90 days · Learn the standard

Absorb the process

Get fluent in the Division's examination workflow, the FFIEC IT Handbook in practice, and how URSIT ratings are reached. Shadow examinations, learn the Report of Examination format, and understand how findings move from fieldwork to write-up.

Months 3–6 · Build the craft

Contribute on exams

Take ownership of assigned URSIT components under guidance, handle vendor and core-processor review areas, and begin pursuing the CISA so my credentials catch up to the work as quickly as possible.

Months 6–12 · Add value

Carry weight on the team

Work examinations with growing independence, support joint exams with FDIC or Federal Reserve counterparts, and bring my technical background to bear where it sharpens the team's assessment of an institution's controls.
